## Two different documents, one source

A clinical guideline and a patient leaflet can describe the exact same treatment and still be, functionally, two different documents. The guideline exists to standardize decisions between clinicians who already share a vocabulary, years of training, and an intuitive sense of how risks stack up against each other. The leaflet exists to help someone with no clinical training decide whether to take a pill, get a scan, or call their doctor back — often while tired, worried, or reading on a phone between other tasks. Treating the second as a shorter version of the first is where a lot of patient materials quietly fail, and it's a failure that's easy to miss because nothing about it looks wrong on the page.

I noticed this most clearly during rotations, watching how much translation happened *in the room* — a clinician reading a guideline-based plan out loud, then immediately re-explaining it in completely different words the moment a patient's face changed. That re-explanation is the actual patient-facing content. The guideline was just the input, the raw material that still needed a second, deliberate pass before it was fit for the person it was ultimately about.

## What gets lost in a direct paraphrase

Three things tend to disappear when a guideline is simply "written down more simply" instead of rebuilt for a patient:

1. **Sequence.** Guidelines are often organized by decision logic (first-line, second-line, contraindications) — a structure that makes sense to someone comparing treatment options against each other. Patients need materials organized by their own timeline instead: what happens today, what happens this week, what happens if something goes wrong. The same facts, reordered around the reader's actual experience rather than the clinician's decision tree.

2. **Weight.** A guideline lists risks with clinical precision, often as a flat list where every item receives roughly equal visual emphasis. A patient needs something different: to know which risk should actually change their behavior versus which one is disclosed for completeness, out of thoroughness rather than urgency. Flattening that distinction is one of the most common ways technically accurate patient materials still manage to mislead by emphasis.

3. **The unasked question.** Guidelines answer "what should a clinician do." They rarely answer "is this my fault," "will this get worse," or "what do I tell my family" — the questions that are actually on a patient's mind, running underneath whatever they're nodding along to. A document that never addresses them leaves a reader to answer those questions on their own, usually by assuming the worst.

## A small example

A guideline might state a medication "is contraindicated in patients with hepatic impairment; monitor liver function periodically." A patient leaflet that just simplifies the vocabulary might say: "Don't take this if you have liver problems. Your doctor will check your liver sometimes." That's not wrong, but it leaves the reader with an open question sitting right behind it: *does that mean something's already wrong with me?* Better patient-facing language answers the next question directly, before it has to be asked: "Your doctor already checked this before prescribing it. You'll have a blood test every few months to make sure your liver is handling the medication well — this is routine, not a sign that something is wrong."

Same fact. Completely different job. The clinical content hasn't changed at all — what's changed is which question the sentence is actually answering.

## Why this matters for medical writing

This is, in miniature, the whole discipline: clinical accuracy is the floor, not the ceiling. Getting the facts right is necessary and non-negotiable, but it's also the easy part, in the sense that it can be checked against a source document. The real work starts after the facts are confirmed, in deciding what a specific reader needs to walk away knowing — and writing toward that goal, not toward the shape of the source document that happened to supply the information. A guideline and a patient leaflet can be built from the same evidence and still require two entirely separate acts of writing, because they're answering two entirely different questions for two entirely different readers.
