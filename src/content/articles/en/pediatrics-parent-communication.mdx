## The audience is never just the patient

In pediatrics, the person you're explaining a diagnosis to is almost never the person who's sick. A toddler with a fever doesn't need the explanation — the exhausted parent standing next to the crib does, and that parent is reading far more into your tone and word choice than the actual clinical content in front of them. During my pediatric rotations, I noticed that the same clinical fact could land as reassuring or terrifying depending entirely on how it was framed, not on what it actually was. Two clinicians could deliver identical information and produce entirely different levels of parental panic, purely through word order and emphasis.

That distinction — between what is true and how it's *received* — turned out to be the most transferable lesson I took from those rotations into writing. It's a distinction that's easy to state and surprisingly hard to internalize, because a clinically accurate sentence feels finished the moment the facts are correct, even when it hasn't yet done the actual communicative work required.

## Parents are triaging your words, not just the diagnosis

A parent hearing "your child has a viral infection, it should resolve on its own" is simultaneously processing several unspoken questions: *Did I do something wrong? Should I be more worried than the doctor sounds? What exactly am I watching for?* If those questions go unaddressed, a parent will often fill the silence with the worst-case interpretation — not because they're irrational, but because uncertainty defaults to worry when a child is involved, and an unanswered question left hanging in a medical conversation rarely resolves itself toward the calm interpretation.

Good pediatric communication answers the unspoken question before it has to be asked out loud. Instead of just "this should resolve on its own," a clearer version does more work:

> "This is a common virus, and your child's body is already fighting it well — that's exactly what the fever is doing. You don't need antibiotics for this; they wouldn't help. What *would* worry us: if the fever lasts more than three days, or your child becomes difficult to wake."

Same clinical fact. But it closes the loop a worried parent is actually holding open, and it does something else worth naming explicitly: it gives the parent a clear, actionable threshold for when to worry, which is often more reassuring than a blanket "don't worry" that leaves them without any way to judge their own situation later, alone, at 2am.

## Puériculture: teaching care before there's a problem

Puériculture — guidance on feeding, growth, sleep, and development in the well child — is a different kind of writing challenge than acute illness. There's no urgency to anchor the reader's attention, and the risk is different: not panic, but the quiet erosion of confidence that comes from too much conflicting advice online, delivered by sources with no way to be held accountable for being wrong. Here the job isn't to calm a fear but to build a parent's trust in their own judgment, one clear, evidence-based answer at a time — "yes, this is normal," said plainly and often, does more for a new parent's confidence than a comprehensive but overwhelming guide ever will, because confidence is built through repeated small confirmations, not through a single exhaustive document consulted once and then forgotten.

This matters because a first-time parent isn't just looking for facts; they're looking for a stable, trustworthy voice among many competing ones, and the tone of a piece of writing does real work in establishing which voice that will be for them, long before the specific facts are even evaluated.

## Why this matters for medical writing

Pediatric communication made visible something true of all patient-facing writing, just more sharply, because the anxiety involved when a child is the patient tends to be less filtered, more visible, and less easily reasoned away than adult anxiety about one's own health: you are rarely writing for a neutral reader. You're writing for someone already carrying some anxiety into the page, whether or not it shows in how they phrase their question, and the actual skill is addressing that anxiety directly, rather than hoping clear facts alone will quietly dissolve it on their own, without acknowledgment.
