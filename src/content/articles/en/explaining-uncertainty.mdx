## Certainty is comfortable, and usually inaccurate

Across rotations in internal medicine, neurology, cardiology, and emergency medicine, one pattern repeated more than any specific diagnosis: the confident-sounding answer was rarely the accurate one. A differential diagnosis is, by definition, a list of possibilities ranked by likelihood — not a single answer waiting to be revealed. Learning to sit inside that ambiguity, rather than resolving it prematurely for the sake of a tidy explanation, turned out to be one of the more transferable skills from clinical training.

It's a strange thing to get comfortable with. Medical training spends years teaching pattern recognition — this constellation of symptoms usually means this — and then, quietly, teaches something harder underneath it: that "usually" is doing enormous work in that sentence, and a good clinician never lets themselves forget it. The instinct to reach for a clean, singular answer is strong, and it's strongest exactly when a patient (or a reader) wants one most badly.

## Why this matters for writing

Medical content has a strong pull toward false confidence, because certainty reads as more authoritative and is easier to write. A sentence like "this treatment works" is simpler to construct than "this treatment works for a defined subset of patients, under specific conditions, with effect sizes that vary by severity" — and it's tempting to reach for the simpler version, especially when writing for a general audience where nuance is assumed to be unwelcome. But a well-written piece of health content should carry the same honesty a good clinician carries into a consultation: "here's what the evidence supports, here's what it doesn't yet resolve, and here's what that means for you practically."

That distinction shows up in phrasing more than most writers expect:

- "Studies show X" flattens a body of evidence into a single verdict, implying a settled consensus that may not exist.
- "The current evidence suggests X, though results vary by population" keeps the uncertainty visible without becoming unreadable or academic.
- "This works" collapses a nuanced effect size into a binary that a reader will remember as more absolute than it actually is.
- "This helps most people in X situation, though not everyone" gives a reader an accurate mental model they can actually apply to their own case.

The skill isn't hedging every sentence into vagueness — an article that qualifies everything becomes just as unusable as one that overstates everything. The skill is knowing precisely which uncertainty is load-bearing for the reader's decision, and preserving exactly that one, while writing everything else with full confidence.

## A concrete example

Presenting a clinical case at Congressis — a breast cancer diagnosis during pregnancy — meant working through a scenario with no single correct treatment path, only a best-available balance of maternal and fetal risk depending on gestational stage. Explaining that case well to a room of physicians meant naming the uncertainty explicitly, not resolving it into a false "and then they did X, and it worked" narrative. A trimester-dependent decision tree, chemotherapy timing constrained by fetal development, staging decisions complicated by pregnancy-altered imaging options — none of it collapses into a single "correct" answer, and pretending otherwise for the sake of a cleaner presentation would have misrepresented the actual clinical reasoning involved.

The same principle scales down to a one-page patient handout. A patient reading about a new diagnosis doesn't need the full decision tree — but they do need to know, honestly, which parts of their treatment plan are well-established and which parts are being adjusted based on how they personally respond. Blurring that line doesn't make the content more reassuring; it just means the patient is unprepared the first time their treatment plan changes, because nothing signaled to them that a change was ever a normal possibility.

## Takeaway

Good medical writing doesn't remove uncertainty to make content feel more authoritative — it structures uncertainty so it stops feeling like a wall and starts feeling like usable information. That's a discipline, not a stylistic preference: it requires knowing the evidence well enough to identify exactly where the genuine open questions are, and then having the writing skill to present that honestly without losing the reader's confidence along the way. Six years of sitting with differential diagnoses that never resolved into neat certainty is, as it turns out, fairly direct training for exactly that.
