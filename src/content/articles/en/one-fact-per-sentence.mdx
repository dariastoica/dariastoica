## The rule

One fact per sentence. That's the whole rule. If a sentence contains a diagnosis *and* a mechanism *and* a caveat *and* an instruction, at least three of those four things will not survive to the reader's understanding — not because the reader is careless, but because working memory has hard limits, and a sentence carrying four separate ideas is asking a lot of it, especially from a reader who's tired, worried, or reading in a waiting room.

This sounds almost too simple to matter, the kind of advice that gets nodded at and then ignored. In practice, it's one of the most reliable edits I make to a draft, and it's rarely the vocabulary that's the problem — it's the density.

## Before and after

Here's a sentence that could plausibly appear in a real patient handout, unedited:

> "Since your blood pressure is a bit elevated, which can sometimes happen due to stress, diet, or other factors, and can increase your risk of heart problems over time if left unmanaged, we'd like you to try reducing your salt intake and come back in three months for a recheck."

That's one sentence carrying: a finding, three possible causes, a long-term risk, an instruction, and a follow-up plan. Nothing in it is wrong. All of it is true. And a reader skimming it — which most readers are, most of the time, especially in a health context where anxiety narrows attention rather than sharpening it — will likely retain none of it cleanly. By the time they reach the instruction at the end, the finding at the beginning has already faded.

Split by fact:

> "Your blood pressure reading today was a bit high. This can happen for a lot of reasons and doesn't mean anything is seriously wrong right now. Two things will help: eating less salt, and coming back in three months so we can check it again."

Same information, in the same order of importance. Four short sentences instead of one long one. Each sentence does one job, and each job is finished before the next one starts.

## Why this works

The mechanism isn't really about sentence length — a short sentence carrying two compressed ideas is just as hard to parse as a long one carrying four. It's about matching the structure of the writing to the structure of how people actually process new information under mild stress, and almost everyone reading a health document is under at least mild stress, whether or not it shows. One idea, confirmed, then the next idea, confirmed. A sentence that tries to do four things at once forces the reader to hold all four in suspension until the period lands, hoping they can sort out afterward which part mattered most — and that's exactly the kind of cognitive load that health literacy research consistently flags as a barrier to comprehension and recall, independent of a reader's education level or general reading ability.

There's a secondary benefit that's easy to miss: splitting facts apart also splits *decisions* apart. A reader can agree with "eat less salt" without yet having processed "come back in three months," and vice versa — each becomes a separate, smaller commitment instead of one large, vague one to be sorted out later.

## Where to use it, and where not to

This rule is for patient-facing writing, not clinical documentation exchanged between professionals, where density and precision serve a different, faster-moving reader who can hold more in mind at once because the vocabulary is already load-bearing for them. Compressing four ideas into one sentence for a colleague who already shares the context isn't a failure of clarity; it's efficient. The skill worth building isn't "always write short sentences" — it's knowing which document you're writing for, and defaulting to one fact per sentence specifically when the reader on the other end is a patient, not a colleague, and has no shared professional shorthand to lean on.
