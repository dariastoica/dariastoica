## The framing problem

"Low health literacy" is usually described as something a patient *has* — a deficit located in the reader, something to be measured, flagged, and worked around. But sit through enough discharge conversations, as I did throughout clinical rotations, and a different pattern becomes obvious: the same patient who can't parse a discharge summary can read a mortgage document, follow a recipe, or explain a complicated family situation in detail, unprompted, with real precision. The literacy isn't missing. The writing didn't meet it halfway.

This reframing matters more than it sounds like it should. If the problem lives in the patient, the solution is education — teach people to read better documents. If the problem lives in the writing, the solution is different: write better documents. Those two framings point toward completely different interventions, and for the last two decades, health systems have mostly invested in the first one while the second one sat comparatively neglected, despite being the one actually within a writer's control.

## Where clinical writing loses the reader

Three habits show up constantly in patient-facing materials, each one solvable at the sentence level, without any loss of clinical accuracy:

- **Passive voice hides the actor.** "The medication should be taken twice daily" doesn't say *who* is responsible for making that happen, and a tired reader skimming past it can walk away without registering that the instruction applies to them, specifically, starting today. "Take this medication twice a day" does the same clinical job while leaving no ambiguity about who's supposed to act.

- **Diagnostic vocabulary substitutes for explanation.** Naming a condition is not the same as explaining what it means for someone's day-to-day life. A patient can hear "atrial fibrillation" ten times across three appointments and still not know whether it's an emergency, whether it's permanent, or whether it means anything about how long they'll live — because naming something and explaining it are two different acts, and clinical writing frequently does only the first while assuming it has accomplished the second.

- **Information is sequenced for the writer's logic, not the reader's.** Clinical documentation follows a diagnostic workflow — history, findings, assessment, plan — because that's the order that makes sense to someone building a clinical picture. A patient reading it isn't building a clinical picture; they want the plan first: what happens next, and what they personally need to do about it. Everything else is context they'll read if the plan makes sense to them, not before.

## What actually helps

None of this requires dumbing content down — a common and reasonable fear among clinicians asked to simplify their writing. It requires reordering and re-anchoring content around the reader's actual question rather than the writer's internal logic. In practice, that means:

1. **Leading with the action or takeaway, not the mechanism.** State what the reader needs to do or know first; explain the reasoning behind it second, for the reader who wants it.
2. **Naming the diagnosis once, then never relying on it to carry meaning again.** A label introduced once should be explained in plain terms immediately after — explain, don't just label, and don't assume the label will do explanatory work on repeat mentions.
3. **Writing the sentence a nervous person would actually be able to hold in their head after reading it once.** This is a genuinely useful editing test: if a sentence needs to be re-read to be understood, it usually needs to be rewritten, not just re-read more carefully.

## The stakes are not abstract

Poorly written patient materials don't just create momentary confusion — they create real, measurable gaps in medication adherence, follow-up attendance, and clinical outcomes, gaps that get quietly attributed to "non-compliance" rather than to the document that failed to communicate what compliance actually required. The fix for a meaningful share of that problem isn't more information, delivered in more channels, with more repetition. It's better-written information, delivered once, clearly: this is, functionally, an editorial problem before it's a medical one, and it's solvable with the same rigor and attention any other piece of writing deserves.
