**Note:** This is a demonstration writing sample created for this portfolio, modeled on the kind of vaccine information sheets used in pediatric and family medicine settings — not a piece commissioned by an employer.

## Brief

Parents deciding on a vaccination schedule are often reading in short bursts, between other tasks, and sometimes after encountering conflicting information online that arrived with more emotional urgency than the clinical materials it's competing with. The goal of this sample is a one-page sheet that states clearly what the MMR vaccine does, what to expect afterward, and addresses the two concerns parents raise most often — without being defensive or dismissive about those concerns, since defensiveness tends to read as evidence of something to hide, regardless of intent.

## Sample excerpt

> **The MMR vaccine protects against measles, mumps, and rubella — three serious viral infections.**
>
> Your child gets two doses: the first around 12–15 months, the second between 4–6 years. Both doses together give strong, long-lasting protection — most people who receive both doses are protected for life.
>
> **What to expect after the shot:** Mild soreness at the injection site, and sometimes a low fever or a faint rash 7–10 days later. This is your child's immune system responding as expected, not a sign that something is wrong. These reactions are also a good sign the vaccine is doing exactly what it's supposed to do.
>
> **"Is this the vaccine linked to autism?"** No. This claim came from a single study in 1998 that was later retracted for fraud, and its author lost his medical license. Every large study conducted since — involving millions of children, across multiple countries and research teams with no connection to each other — has found no link between the MMR vaccine and autism.
>
> **"Why does my child need this if the disease is rare here?"** Measles is rare specifically *because* vaccination rates are high. When vaccination rates drop, measles comes back quickly — it's one of the most contagious viruses known, capable of spreading in a room hours after an infected person has left it.
>
> **"Can my child get the diseases from the vaccine itself?"** No. The MMR vaccine uses weakened, not live, disease-causing viruses — it cannot cause measles, mumps, or rubella in a healthy child.

## Approach

Three decisions shaped this piece:

1. **Naming the concern before dismissing it.** Parents who've encountered the autism claim need to see it acknowledged directly, not ignored — silence reads as evasion, not confidence, and a document that avoids the topic entirely often makes a worried parent trust it less, not more.
2. **Explaining mechanism briefly, not just asserting safety.** "No link found" lands better paired with *why* rates stay low (high vaccination coverage) and *why* the original claim was wrong (a retracted, fraudulent study), rather than as a bare reassurance asking to be taken on faith.
3. **Anticipating the second-most-common question, not just the first.** Parents who accept the autism answer often have a follow-up question about the vaccine itself causing illness; addressing it proactively, in the same document, avoids sending them back to search engines for an answer that may not be as carefully sourced.

## Why this matters for medical writing

Vaccine communication is one of the clearest cases where tone determines whether accurate information actually reaches someone. Facts alone don't move a worried parent — facts delivered without judgment, that treat the question as reasonable to ask, do. The goal isn't to win an argument; it's to be the most trustworthy document a parent reads on the subject, which requires taking their actual questions seriously rather than only the questions a document would prefer to answer.
