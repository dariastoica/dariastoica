**Note:** This is a demonstration writing sample created for this portfolio, based on the kind of patient-facing explanation I gave regularly during clinical rotations — not a piece commissioned by an employer.

## Brief

Patients leaving a diagnostic appointment retain very little of what's said to them verbally, particularly when the diagnosis is new and anxiety-inducing — a well-documented pattern that has less to do with a patient's attentiveness and more to do with how acute stress narrows working memory during exactly the conversation where the most needs to be absorbed. The goal of this sample is a one-page explainer that could accompany a Type 2 diabetes diagnosis: no jargon, no fear-based framing, just the essential "what does this mean and what happens next," written so it can survive being read exactly once, on the drive home, by someone who is not at their calmest.

## Sample excerpt

> **You've been diagnosed with type 2 diabetes. Here's what that actually means.**
>
> Your body isn't using insulin as well as it used to, which means sugar builds up in your blood instead of moving into your cells for energy. It's a manageable condition, not an emergency — but it does need attention starting now.
>
> **What changes today:** nothing drastic. Your care team will likely start with one medication and a few changes to meals and activity — not a complete overhaul overnight. Most people are managing this well within a few months of adjustment, not living in a permanent state of restriction.
>
> **What you'll want to track:** your blood sugar readings, at the times your doctor recommends, so your next appointment can adjust your plan based on real data instead of guesswork. A small notebook or a phone app both work fine — the format matters far less than doing it consistently.
>
> **What this doesn't mean:** it doesn't mean you did something wrong. Type 2 diabetes develops from a mix of genetics, age, and metabolism — not from a single food or habit, and no one factor you can name is "the reason" this happened.
>
> **What to expect at your next visit:** your care team will look at your tracked readings together with you, and adjust the plan if needed. This is a normal, ongoing process, not a sign that the first plan failed.

## Approach

Four structural choices carried this piece:

1. **Leading with reassurance, not statistics.** A patient in the first hour of a new diagnosis isn't ready for prevalence numbers or long-term complication rates — they need to know the ground under them hasn't disappeared before they can absorb anything else.
2. **Answering the unasked question.** "What did I do wrong" is almost always present, even unspoken, in the minutes after a new diagnosis. Addressing it directly, briefly, and without being asked removes a layer of anxiety that would otherwise interfere with reading the rest of the document at all.
3. **Action before explanation.** Patients remember what to *do* far better than mechanisms of disease; the "why" is included, but after the practical guidance, so the most actionable information isn't competing with background explanation for the reader's limited attention.
4. **Previewing the next visit.** Telling a patient what to expect at the follow-up appointment turns an unknown, potentially anxiety-provoking future event into something familiar and already partly understood before it happens.

## Why this matters for medical writing

This is the core discipline of patient education content: every sentence has to work for someone reading it once, under stress, without a clinician in the room to answer follow-up questions or notice a confused expression and re-explain. There's no second chance to clarify a sentence that didn't land, which means the writing itself has to do the entire job a good clinical conversation would otherwise do in real time — anticipating confusion before it happens, rather than responding to it after the fact.
