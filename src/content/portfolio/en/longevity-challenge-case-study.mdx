**Note:** This piece is a portfolio write-up reflecting on my participation as part of the Stanford Challenge, Longevity Design track (2023), *"Optimizing Health Span: Living Well at Every Age."* It summarizes the health-communication angle of that experience rather than reproducing any official challenge materials.

## Brief

The Stanford Challenge brought together participants from medicine, design, and technology backgrounds to work on a shared question: not how to extend lifespan, but how to extend *health span* — the years lived in good functional health, not just the years lived. Teams were expected to move quickly from concept to prototype, working across disciplines that don't share a default vocabulary, which made communication itself as much a constraint on the work as any technical limitation. My contribution centered on translating a clinical distinction that's intuitive to medical professionals but often collapsed in public conversation: living longer and living well are not the same design problem, and solutions built for one don't automatically serve the other.

## Core contribution

> Most public health messaging around aging defaults to a single frame — "add more years" — that quietly assumes those years will be healthy ones, an assumption that clinical data doesn't actually support as a given outcome of longer life. Clinically, health span and lifespan diverge more than popular framing suggests: a person can gain years of life while losing years of independent function, and interventions that extend one don't reliably extend the other — a medication that reduces mortality risk, for instance, says nothing on its own about whether those additional years will be spent mobile and cognitively intact. Reframing the design question around health span rather than lifespan changes what counts as a good solution — prioritizing mobility, cognitive function, and independence over raw longevity metrics that are easier to measure but less connected to what people actually want from additional years of life.

## Approach

Three things shaped how I communicated this distinction to a non-clinical, interdisciplinary team:

1. **Making the distinction concrete, not abstract.** "Health span vs. lifespan" means little as a phrase alone; grounding it in a relatable example — two people living to the same age with very different final decades, one independent and mobile, the other not — gave design-focused teammates something tangible to design around, rather than an abstract clinical distinction to take on faith.
2. **Translating clinical priorities into design constraints.** Rather than presenting a lecture on geriatric medicine, the goal was framing clinical priorities (mobility, cognition, independence) as concrete design targets a non-clinical team could actually build toward and measure progress against.
3. **Staying available for iterative clarification, not just an initial briefing.** As the team's prototypes developed, new clinical questions surfaced repeatedly; treating the health-communication role as ongoing, rather than a single upfront explanation, kept the clinical framing accurate as the design work evolved in directions the initial briefing hadn't anticipated.

## Why this matters for medical writing

Interdisciplinary collaboration is where medical communication is tested most directly: there's no shared clinical vocabulary to lean on, and a concept that isn't translated clearly simply doesn't make it into the final design, no matter how sound the underlying clinical reasoning was. This experience reinforced that the same discipline used in patient education — finding the framing that makes a clinical idea land, and staying responsive as new questions arise — applies just as directly when the audience is engineers and designers instead of patients, and that the cost of unclear communication is just as real in both settings, even if it shows up differently: a missed feature instead of a missed diagnosis, but a communication failure either way.
