**Note:** This is a demonstration writing sample created for this portfolio, modeled on patient-facing recruitment materials used to introduce clinical trials before the formal informed consent process — not a piece commissioned by an employer or sponsor.

## Brief

Recruitment materials sit in an unusual position: they need to generate genuine interest in participation while being scrupulously honest about time commitment, possible side effects, and the fact that a trial is research, not guaranteed treatment. The goal of this sample is a recruitment page for a hypothetical Phase 3 study of an add-on medication for type 2 diabetes — written to inform a decision, not to persuade past legitimate hesitation.

## Sample excerpt

> **Could a new medication help manage your type 2 diabetes? We're looking for volunteers to help us find out.**
>
> This research study is testing whether a new medication, taken alongside your current treatment, can help more people reach their target blood sugar levels. You may be eligible if you're 18–75, have been diagnosed with type 2 diabetes for at least one year, and your current treatment isn't fully controlling your blood sugar.
>
> **What participation involves:** 8 clinic visits over 12 months, blood sugar monitoring, and either the study medication or a placebo — neither you nor your study doctor will know which one you're receiving until the study ends. This is standard practice that helps researchers get a clear answer.
>
> **What you should know before deciding:** This is research, not a guarantee of improved blood sugar control. Some participants may experience side effects; these will be explained in full, along with all other details, during the informed consent process — before you agree to anything. You can leave the study at any time, for any reason, without it affecting your regular care.
>
> **What you'll get either way:** close monitoring of your diabetes by the study team throughout the trial, and access to the results once the study concludes.
>
> **Who is running this study:** an independent research team, overseen by an ethics review board whose sole job is to protect participants' rights and safety — a board with no financial stake in the study's outcome, and the authority to pause or stop the trial if new information suggests it should.
>
> **What happens after the study ends:** if the medication is shown to help, you may be offered the option to continue receiving it. If it isn't, you'll return to your standard care plan, with no disruption to the relationship with your regular care team.

## Approach

Four choices shaped this piece, all driven by the same principle: interest should come from clear information, not from omission.

1. **Naming the placebo arm plainly, early.** Burying this detail — or describing it vaguely — is a common way recruitment copy misleads by omission. Stating it upfront, with the reason behind it, builds trust rather than eroding it.
2. **Separating "what you'll know later" from "what you need to know now."** Full risk disclosure belongs in the informed consent document, not the recruitment page — but the recruitment page has to say clearly that fuller disclosure is coming, not skip past it.
3. **A genuine off-ramp.** Stating the right to withdraw at any time, without consequence, isn't just a regulatory requirement — it's the detail that turns "recruitment" into "informed invitation," rather than a one-way pitch designed to close.
4. **Naming the oversight, not just the process.** Mentioning the independent ethics board explicitly, rather than assuming trust in "the research process" as an abstraction, gives a hesitant reader something concrete to weigh: an accountable party whose interests are aligned with the participant's safety, not the study's enrollment target.

## Why this matters for medical writing

Clinical trial recruitment is one of the few areas of medical writing where the ethical bar is explicit and codified — informed consent principles exist precisely because this kind of content has real potential for harm if written to persuade rather than to inform. Writing well here means treating "fully informed" as the actual success metric, not a secondary concern to enrollment numbers.
