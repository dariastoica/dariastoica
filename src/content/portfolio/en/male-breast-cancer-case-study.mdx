**Note:** This piece is a portfolio write-up summarizing my contribution to *"Male breast cancer, evolution in an advanced stage of disease"*, co-authored for Congressis (19th edition), published in the abstract book. It reflects the argument of that work in my own words for demonstration purposes, rather than reproducing the original abstract verbatim.

## Brief

Breast cancer is overwhelmingly framed, in both clinical training and public awareness campaigns, as a disease of women — for good epidemiological reason, since it affects men in fewer than 1% of cases. That rarity has a real downside: because male breast cancer isn't part of routine screening or public awareness the way female breast cancer is, it's disproportionately diagnosed at a later, more advanced stage, when outcomes are harder to change and treatment options are more limited. This piece summarizes the case built around a presentation of male breast cancer at an advanced stage, arguing that its rarity is precisely the reason it needs deliberate clinical attention rather than being treated as a footnote to the female disease model — a footnote that, in practice, translates directly into delayed diagnoses.

## Core argument

> Male breast cancer shares much of its underlying biology with the female disease, but its clinical presentation is shaped by a fundamentally different context: lower baseline suspicion from both patients and clinicians, later presentation, and a body of screening and awareness infrastructure built almost entirely around female patients. The case discussed illustrates how these context factors, not biological aggressiveness alone, can account for a more advanced stage at diagnosis — a man noticing a lump may delay seeking care longer than a woman would, precisely because the possibility of breast cancer doesn't occur to him as readily, and a clinician examining him may not either, for the same reason. This points toward a specific clinical takeaway: any breast mass in a male patient warrants the same diagnostic seriousness as in a female patient, independent of how rare the underlying diagnosis statistically is.

## Approach

Three choices shaped how this case was communicated:

1. **Naming the awareness gap directly, not just the biology.** A purely biological explanation for late diagnosis would miss the actual clinical lesson — the delay is largely a systems and awareness problem, on both the patient and clinician side, and framing it that way makes the takeaway actionable for a clinician's own practice rather than a fact to simply note and move past.
2. **Avoiding "rare, therefore unimportant" framing.** Rarity is exactly why this case needed presenting, not a reason to minimize it — a point made explicitly rather than left to be inferred, since an audience that has internalized "rare" as "low priority" needs that assumption directly challenged, not just contradicted by implication.
3. **Connecting the case to a concrete, repeatable clinical action.** Rather than ending on the observation that delay occurs, the case closed on a specific behavioral takeaway — treat any male breast mass with the same diagnostic seriousness as a female presentation — so the lesson could be applied the next time, not just remembered as an interesting exception.

## Why this matters for medical writing

Rare presentations of common diseases are an area where medical communication has a real, direct effect on outcomes: a clinician who has genuinely absorbed "this can happen, and here's why it's often missed" behaves differently at the next ambiguous presentation than one who has only absorbed statistics about rarity in the abstract, disconnected from what that rarity actually does to a patient's path through the healthcare system. Writing this kind of case well means treating the awareness gap itself as part of the clinical content — not a footnote to it, but the actual point the case exists to make, since the biology alone, however accurately described, wouldn't have explained why the delay happens or what a clinician should do differently as a result. The same principle extends well beyond this specific cancer: any condition assumed to belong to one demographic risks being missed in another, and naming that pattern explicitly is often more valuable teaching than the clinical details of the case itself.
