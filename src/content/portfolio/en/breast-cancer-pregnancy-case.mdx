**Context.** This piece is adapted from a clinical case presentation I authored and delivered at Congressis (19th edition), titled *"Who should be saved? A case of breast cancer during pregnancy,"* which was scored 96.33/100 by the judging panel. The original abstract appears in the Congressis abstract book; this writing sample reworks the case into a clearer explanatory format to demonstrate my approach to structuring dense clinical information for an audience.

## The problem

Breast cancer diagnosed during pregnancy forces clinicians into a narrow decision space: every treatment choice affects two patients — the mother and the fetus — simultaneously and differently depending on gestational age. Delaying treatment protects the pregnancy but may allow disease progression; treating aggressively can protect the mother but carries risks to fetal development, particularly in the first trimester when organogenesis is most vulnerable to chemotherapeutic agents. There's rarely a single "correct" answer, only a best-available balance of risks that shifts as the pregnancy progresses and as new imaging or pathology results arrive.

What makes this scenario genuinely difficult to teach, not just to describe, is that the difficulty isn't located in any single decision — it's distributed across a whole sequence of smaller decisions, each one constraining the ones that follow. A staging decision made in the first trimester affects which treatment options remain viable by the third. Presenting that as a single dramatic dilemma flattens exactly the part that makes it clinically instructive.

## My approach

Rather than presenting the case chronologically (diagnosis → treatment → outcome), I restructured it around the actual decision points clinicians faced — the moments where the treatment plan had to be chosen under incomplete information, with the pregnancy itself as a moving constraint rather than a fixed backdrop. Each decision point was paired with three things, presented in a consistent format so the audience could track the reasoning across the whole case without losing the thread:

- **What the evidence said at that stage** — the actual data available at that point in the timeline, not retrospectively selected evidence that made the eventual choice look more obviously correct than it was in the moment.
- **What made the decision genuinely difficult** — not just "risky," but *why*: which specific competing considerations were in tension, and what made neither option clearly dominant.
- **What was ultimately chosen, and the clinical reasoning behind it** — including the reasoning that was available at the time, not reasoning constructed afterward to justify the outcome.

This structure let the audience follow the logic of the case rather than just its outcome — which is the difference between a case *report*, which mainly documents what happened, and a case that actually teaches something, which shows a listener how to think the next time they face a structurally similar problem with different specifics.

## Result

The presentation scored 96.33/100, and generated discussion afterward about how oncology and obstetric guidelines handle exactly this kind of conflicting-risk scenario — which was the intended outcome: not just informing the room, but giving them a transferable framework for the next time they encounter a similar dilemma, whether or not the specifics matched this exact case. Several audience members raised comparable cases from their own training afterward, which suggested the decision-point structure had done its job of making the reasoning portable rather than case-specific.

## Why this matters for medical writing

Medical writing lives or dies on structure. A reader — whether a physician skimming a case report at a conference or a patient reading a diagnosis explainer at home — needs the logic of a decision made visible, not just its conclusion presented as a fact to accept. Structure is what turns information into something a reader can actually reason with afterward, rather than something they simply absorb and then forget the next time a similar situation requires judgment rather than recall. This is the same underlying skill whether the audience sitting in front of the writing is a conference room full of clinicians or a single patient reading alone in a waiting room, worried about what comes next.
