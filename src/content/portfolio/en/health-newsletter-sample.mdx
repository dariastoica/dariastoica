**Note:** This is a demonstration writing sample created for this portfolio, modeled on a recurring newsletter format a primary care clinic or health-tech company might send to patients — not a piece commissioned by an employer.

## Brief

Newsletters compete with a crowded inbox, so the piece needs to earn attention in the first line, not just inform once someone commits to reading. The goal of this sample is a short newsletter article demystifying blood pressure readings — a topic most people have encountered but few can confidently interpret — written for a general subscriber list, not a clinical audience, with enough personality to be read past the subject line without sacrificing the accuracy a clinic's name has to stand behind.

## Sample excerpt

> **What do those two numbers actually mean?**
>
> You've had your blood pressure checked dozens of times. But if someone asked you to explain what "120 over 80" actually means, would you be able to?
>
> The first number (systolic) measures the pressure in your arteries when your heart beats. The second (diastolic) measures it when your heart rests between beats. Think of it like traffic on a highway: the first number is rush hour, the second is 3am. Both matter — a highway that's only ever congested during rush hour still has a problem, even if the rest of the day looks fine.
>
> **So what's "normal"?** Under 120/80 is considered optimal. Between 120–129 systolic (with diastolic still under 80) is called "elevated" — not high blood pressure yet, but worth watching. 130/80 and above starts to move into the range your care team will want to discuss with you.
>
> **One number spiked. Should you panic?** No single reading tells the whole story — blood pressure naturally shifts with stress, caffeine, and even how you're sitting. What matters is the pattern over time, which is exactly what your regular checkups are tracking.
>
> **A simple habit that actually helps:** if you're monitoring at home, take your reading at the same time each day, seated, after a few minutes of rest — not right after climbing stairs or finishing a coffee. Consistency in *how* you measure matters almost as much as the number itself.

## Approach

Three choices shaped the tone of this piece:

1. **A concrete metaphor over a clinical definition.** "Systolic and diastolic pressure" means little to most readers on its own; the highway comparison gives the two numbers a reason to matter independently, which is the actual point, and it's the kind of image a reader is likely to still remember weeks later.
2. **Defusing single-reading anxiety directly.** Newsletter readers who've had one unexpectedly high reading are a real and anxious segment of the audience; addressing that directly builds more trust than a generic explainer that ignores the reason many people are reading in the first place.
3. **Ending on something actionable, not just informative.** A newsletter that only explains, without giving the reader something small and doable to walk away with, is easy to read and forget; the home-monitoring tip gives this one a reason to be saved rather than skimmed and discarded.

## Why this matters for medical writing

Newsletter content sits at an interesting intersection: it has to be genuinely engaging enough to read past the first sentence, while carrying real clinical accuracy underneath the accessible tone. The skill isn't simplifying the science — it's finding the one framing that makes the science stick, memorable enough to change how a reader interprets their own numbers the next time they see them on a screen at a pharmacy or a doctor's office.
